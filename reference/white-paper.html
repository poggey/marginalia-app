Abstract

Marginalia is a personal reading system with a single conviction at its centre: a good librarian does not guess. It remembers everything you have read, understands why you loved what you loved, and hands you the next book with a confident, defensible reason. This paper specifies a tool that tracks a personal library, captures ratings across multiple dimensions, builds a quantified model of one reader's taste, and recommends the next book by explicit mathematics ‚ÄĒ with a Discovery Range control that lets the reader trade familiarity for adventure.

Version 2.0 replaces the v1.0 design language in full. The librarian remains the concept ‚ÄĒ memory, judgement, transparency, care ‚ÄĒ but the interface is a quiet, modern product: porcelain surfaces, hairline structure, restrained ultramarine, editorial serif reserved for book titles, and a secondary palette of four deep shelf tones. The system opens knowing nothing: every account ‚ÄĒ including the author's own ‚ÄĒ begins as an empty ledger and earns its model through a designed first run (¬ß5.7, ¬ß7.9), so the tool works identically for any reader from day zero. The recommendation mathematics, data model, feature set, architecture and risk register carry forward unchanged in substance and are restated here so this document is the single source of truth for the build.

Chapter I

Vision & principles

Every great library once had a person in it who knew you ‚ÄĒ who noticed what you devoured and what you returned early, and quietly set something aside for your next visit. Marginalia rebuilds that person as software, and gives them a calculator.

The tool has one job with three parts: track everything read (and abandoned), understand the shape of one reader's taste as a measurable object, and recommend the next book with an explicit, inspectable score ‚ÄĒ never a black box, never a shrug. It is a private instrument first; multi-user support is a planned future edition, and every architectural decision here is made so that opening the doors later means adding rooms, not rebuilding foundations.

The librarian is a concept, not a costume. It lives in how the product behaves ‚ÄĒ memory, judgement, transparency, honest uncertainty ‚ÄĒ never in skeuomorphic decoration. The interface is a contemporary product that happens to be about books.

Design principles

  1. The librarian shows their working. Every recommendation arrives with its mathematics attached: the similarity scores, the books that drove it, the confidence interval. A recommendation you can interrogate is one you can trust ‚ÄĒ and correcting it teaches the system.
  2. Low-friction logging or no logging. Adding a finished book must take under fifteen seconds: search, tap, done. Everything else ‚ÄĒ pages, covers, metadata ‚ÄĒ is fetched automatically.
  3. Taste is multi-dimensional. A single five-star scale cannot distinguish ‚Äúbrilliant ideas, cardboard characters‚ÄĚ from ‚Äúgorgeous prose, glacial plot.‚ÄĚ Marginalia rates on axes, because the axes are what the mathematics feeds on.
  4. Negative data is data. A book abandoned at 20% is one of the most informative signals a reader can give. DNFs are first-class records with their own taxonomy of reasons ‚ÄĒ recorded without guilt or theatre.
  5. Comfort is a setting, not a cage. The reader ‚ÄĒ not the algorithm ‚ÄĒ decides how adventurous today's pick should be, via a visible control (the Discovery Range, Chapter VI).
  6. The reader owns the ledger. All data exports in one click as human-readable JSON. No lock-in, no silent cloud dependence.
  7. Quiet confidence. The interface stays out of the way: generous space, few colours, typography doing the work. The one place it raises its voice is the single accent that marks a decision.
A recommendation is a hypothesis about you. Marginalia states the hypothesis, tests it, and updates.
Chapter II

The problem

The reader in question has read a great deal of science fiction and tracked none of it. The record lives in memory, an Audible library, and a vague sense of ‚ÄúI think I've read that one.‚ÄĚ

This creates four concrete failures:

  • The rediscovery problem. Without a ledger, past reading can't inform future choice. What made Project Hail Mary land so hard ‚ÄĒ the voice, the problem-of-the-week structure, the competence, the humour? Untracked, that knowledge decays.
  • The choice paralysis problem. Facing an infinite bookshop, most readers default to whatever a retailer's popularity engine surfaces. Those engines optimise for the average customer and for sales, not for you.
  • The rut problem. Left alone, taste-matching engines converge: love one competent-engineer-solves-space-problems book and you'll be offered forty more, each slightly worse. Discovery requires deliberate, controlled deviation ‚ÄĒ which no mainstream engine hands to the user as a control.
  • The evidence problem. ‚Äú4 stars‚ÄĚ is lossy. Two books can earn it for opposite reasons, and a recommender trained on the aggregate learns almost nothing.

Existing tools each solve a fragment. Goodreads tracks but recommends poorly and is socially noisy. StoryGraph rates on moods but keeps its model opaque. Retail engines recommend but serve the shop. None offer a personal, transparent, mathematically explicit librarian whose only client is one reader. That is the gap Marginalia occupies.

Chapter III

Marginalia is a loop. Books flow in through logging; ratings turn them into signal; the signal shapes a taste model; the model, moderated by the Discovery Range, produces recommendations; reading those produces new ratings; the loop closes and tightens.

Chapter IV

The data model

Everything Marginalia knows lives in four record types: the Book, the Reading Record, the Rating, and the Taste Profile. The first three are facts; the fourth is inference.

4.1 The Book record

A book enters the library via search against the Open Library and Google Books APIs ‚ÄĒ title, author, ISBN, cover, page count, publication year and subject tags arrive automatically. On top of the fetched metadata, Marginalia attaches its own profile: the numerical description the mathematics operates on.

4.2 The book profile: twelve tone axes

Raw genre tags are too coarse (‚Äúscience fiction‚ÄĚ describes both Project Hail Mary and Dhalgren, which share almost nothing). Marginalia describes every book along twelve tone axes, each 0 to 1, plus a bag of theme tags. Axis values are seeded automatically ‚ÄĒ from subject tags, series data and a lookup table of common titles ‚ÄĒ then adjustable by the reader, because the reader's perception is the ground truth that matters.

Any reader's loves and aversions receive the same treatment ‚ÄĒ the space carries no assumptions about whose taste will land in it.

4.3 The Reading Record

A Reading Record is one encounter between the reader and a book. Re-reads create new records ‚ÄĒ a book loved at 19 and re-read at 25 is two data points, and the model weights the recent one more heavily.

  • Status: queued ‚Üí reading ‚Üí finished | abandoned | paused. Paused books prompt gently after 30 days: shelve or resume.
  • Format: audio / print / ebook, with sessions optionally logged (date, minutes or pages) to power pace statistics.
  • Dates: started, finished. From these: days-to-finish, hours-per-week, seasonal patterns.
  • DNF taxonomy: abandonment asks for one reason from a fixed list ‚ÄĒ pacing, tone, characters, prose, lost interest, too grim, too confusing, wrong mood (no fault). ‚ÄúWrong mood‚ÄĚ is crucial: it tells the model to suppress the book, not to punish its features. The UI treats stopping as neutral information: ‚ÄúStopped at 31%‚ÄĚ, quiet grey, no theatre.

4.4 The Rating record ‚ÄĒ six axes and a verdict

On finishing, the Review flow asks for five quick axis scores (0‚Äď10 sliders), one overall verdict (half-star precision, 0.5‚Äď5.0), and one binary question that carries enormous signal: ‚ÄúWould you re-read this?‚ÄĚ

4.5 The Taste Profile

The Taste Profile is not entered by anyone; it is computed ‚ÄĒ a weighted centroid in the twelve-axis space, plus learned per-axis sensitivities. It is fully visible in the Atlas as the taste radar, and fully recomputable from the ledger at any time. Delete it and it regrows. This is what makes the eventual multi-user edition cheap: a new user is simply an empty ledger and a fresh profile.

Chapter V

The mathematics of taste

This is the engine room. The recommender is content-based, transparent, and built to work well from roughly fifteen rated books ‚ÄĒ because a personal tool cannot wait for the thousands of users that collaborative filtering needs. Five stages: represent, weigh, compare, predict, select.

5.6 Hard filters and standing rules

Before any scoring, candidates pass through deterministic filters the reader controls directly: already-read (unless re-read mode), series entries out of order (book 3 never offered before book 1), runtime window (default 8‚Äď14 audio hours, adjustable), active suppressions, and any standing rule the reader writes (‚Äúnever recommend book one of an unfinished trilogy‚ÄĚ is expected to be rule number one). Filters are visible and editable in settings ‚ÄĒ the librarian's standing orders.

5.7 Cold start: day zero

Every account starts at exactly zero. There is no pre-loaded history, no assumed taste, no special path for any particular reader ‚ÄĒ the author's own account is created the same way as anyone else's. On first run the taste vector does not exist, and the first-run flow (¬ß7.9) offers three ways to create it, usable in any mix:

  • Calibration. The reader is dealt ~20 well-known SF titles spanning the twelve axes ‚ÄĒ Weir to Le Guin, Bobiverse to Blindsight ‚ÄĒ and rates the ones they know, skipping freely. Even a dozen quick ratings sketch the taste polygon (Appendix XIII lists the deck and its selection criteria).
  • Backfill. The reader logs books they have actually read, in a rapid-fire flow built for exactly this: search, tap the rough year read, drop a verdict, next. Real history is the highest-quality signal the model can get.
  • Stated preferences. Likes and aversions the reader simply knows ‚ÄĒ ‚Äúno military SF‚ÄĚ, ‚Äúnothing over 20 hours‚ÄĚ, ‚Äúlove engineering puzzles‚ÄĚ ‚ÄĒ entered as standing preferences and hard filters (¬ß5.6), never as fake ratings. These constrain recommendations immediately, before any ratings exist.

Below roughly ten rated books, the model is honest about its ignorance: the For You surface carries a visible Learning state, predictions show deliberately wide bands, picks lean on stated preferences and broadly loved titles labelled as such, and a progress line makes the contract explicit ‚ÄĒ ‚ÄúRate 6 more books to unlock confident predictions.‚ÄĚ Trust is built by admitting what the model does not yet know.

5.8 Learning per-axis sensitivity

Not every axis matters equally to every reader. Once ~25 ratings exist, Marginalia fits a small ridge regression of verdicts on tone axes to learn which dimensions actually drive this reader's joy ‚ÄĒ e.g., discovering that pace and problem_solving predict their verdicts strongly while prose_style barely registers. The learned sensitivities re-weight the similarity metric and are displayed openly in the Atlas as ‚ÄúWhat actually moves you.‚ÄĚ The mathematics stops being a formula about books and becomes a statement about the reader.

Chapter VI

The Discovery Range

The Discovery Range is the reader's hand on the tiller ‚ÄĒ a single visible control that answers the question every recommender silently decides for you: how far from home should tonight's book be?

Mechanically it is one number, őī, feeding the scoring formula of ¬ß5.5. In the interface it is a clean five-stop slider ‚ÄĒ a hairline track, a filled segment, a ringed current stop ‚ÄĒ with each position a named posture rather than a percentage:

Behavioural details

  • The wildcard slot. At Far and Unknown, the fifth shortlist position is reserved for a pure-novelty pick: high nov(c), prediction honestly shown as unscored, marked with a small Wildcard tag. It is framed as a dare, not a promise.
  • Range accounting. Every recommendation records the őī at which it was issued. Over time the panel beneath the control shows the reader's real average rating at the selected range ‚ÄĒ ‚Äú4.3 avg across 61 books‚ÄĚ ‚ÄĒ so the choice is informed by their own evidence. If Far-range picks average 4.2, the reader learns something true: their comfort zone was drawn too small.
  • No silent drift. The system never changes őī on its own. If six consecutive picks came from one tight cluster it may suggest widening the range ‚ÄĒ one quiet line, dismissible ‚ÄĒ but the hand on the control is always the reader's.
Chapter VII

Features & surfaces

Chapter III named the surfaces; this chapter furnishes them. Each subsection specifies what the surface does and the small decisions that make it pleasant rather than merely functional.

7.1 Library

Every book ever logged, as a clean card grid: cover block (real cover, or a shelf-tone placeholder with the title set in serif), title, author, rating, format. Hovering a card reveals its profile ‚ÄĒ the tone-axis bars ‚ÄĒ in a quiet white overlay; clicking opens the full book page with the reader's six-axis rating, mood tags, dates, notes, and the editable tone axes. Search is instant across every field; filters combine (author, series, year read, rating range, mood, format, discovery range at recommendation). A dense table view sits one keystroke away (V) for when the reader wants the ledger, not the shelf.

7.2 For you

The recommendation surface, and the product's centrepiece. At the top, the hero pick ‚ÄĒ one book, not a wall of covers:

Whatever is open right now: progress (hours listened or pages turned), a pace sparkline, and a projected finish date computed from the last fortnight's pace ‚ÄĒ quietly useful for deciding whether the next book fits before a trip. To-be-read with two honest mechanics. The inverse surface: books and regions of taste-space the model predicts the reader would dislike, each with its reasoning shown. Two purposes. 7.9 First run

The first-run flow is the product's front door and, in the eventual public edition, every user's first impression ‚ÄĒ so it is specified as carefully as any surface. It must take a brand-new reader from an empty ledger to a first defensible recommendation in under ten minutes, without ever pretending to know more than it does.

The first run ‚ÄĒ four steps, ~5‚Äď10 minutes
  1. Welcome & the contract. One screen, three sentences: what Marginalia tracks, how it recommends, and that everything lives in the reader's own ledger and exports freely. No account jargon, no tour.
  2. What do you already know about your taste? Optional stated preferences as tappable chips plus free text ‚ÄĒ aversions (‚Äúno military SF‚ÄĚ), constraints (‚Äúaudiobooks, 8‚Äď14 hours‚ÄĚ), loves (‚Äúengineering puzzles‚ÄĚ). Stored as standing preferences and filters (¬ß5.6). Skippable entirely.
  3. Rate what you recognise. The calibration deck: ~20 covers dealt one at a time, half-star rating or a single-tap skip; a live counter shows the taste polygon forming in miniature as ratings land ‚ÄĒ the model visibly taking shape is the reward for the effort. The reader can stop at any point.
  4. Backfill, if you like. An invitation into rapid-fire logging of real history ‚ÄĒ search, year read, verdict, next ‚ÄĒ framed as optional and resumable from the Library at any time.

The flow ends on For You in its Learning state (¬ß5.7): a first pick with honest wide bands, the Discovery Range live, and a progress line stating exactly what will sharpen the model. Every step after the welcome is skippable ‚ÄĒ a reader who skips everything simply starts with a blank model, popular-safe suggestions labelled as such, and the same path to precision as everyone else. Nothing in the system depends on who the reader is; the first run is the universal onboarding, which is why the public edition (¬ß10.3) requires no new design.

Chapter IX

Design system: Porcelain & Ink

The design language is called Porcelain & Ink: a quiet, modern product surface ‚ÄĒ porcelain white, hairline structure, near-black ink ‚ÄĒ with one confident ultramarine accent and a secondary palette of four deep shelf tones. The librarian lives in the behaviour and the words; the visual language never dresses up as anything.

9.1 Why this direction

Version 1.0 borrowed the physical card catalogue ‚ÄĒ stamps, ruled paper, mahogany drawers ‚ÄĒ and it read as costume: janky, effortful, fake. The lesson is recorded here so it is not relearned: the concept belongs in the logic, not the chrome. A tool whose whole point is honest mathematics should look the way honest things look ‚ÄĒ clean, calm, legible, with warmth carried by typography and the books themselves rather than by props. The single permitted literary note is the serif: book titles, big numbers and pull-quotes are set in Newsreader, everything functional in Instrument Sans. The books' covers (and, where covers are absent, the shelf tones) provide all the richness the surfaces deliberately withhold.

9.6 Motion

Motion is functional and brief: it confirms cause and effect, never performs.

  • Shortlist re-rank: rows fade/translate in with a 45ms stagger when the Discovery Range moves ‚ÄĒ the one orchestrated moment, because it teaches the control.
  • Hover: cards lift 3px with shadow; buttons lift 1px. 150‚Äď200ms ease.
  • The range control: fill animates with a soft spring (300ms, small overshoot).
  • Everything else: instant or a 150ms fade. Full prefers-reduced-motion fallback to crossfades.

9.7 Dark appearance

Deferred, deliberately. If added, it derives from the shelf tones ‚ÄĒ Graphite surfaces with Midnight accents, warm off-white text ‚ÄĒ rather than inverting the porcelain palette. It must be designed as its own appearance, not generated; until then the product ships light-only.

Chapter X

Technical architecture

Local-first, free-tier, zero-backend-until-needed. The entire v1 runs in the browser, costs nothing to host, and keeps the reader's ledger on the reader's machine ‚ÄĒ with the seams for a multi-user edition already cut.

10.2 The candidate pool

A recommender needs books it can recommend from. Sources, in order: (1) the reader's own Queue; (2) a curated seed corpus of ~500 notable SF titles with hand-checked profiles, shipped with the app ‚ÄĒ quality over coverage; (3) on-demand expansion: Open Library subject and author queries around high-scoring regions of taste-space, with fetched candidates receiving auto-seeded profiles marked Unverified until the reader confirms or adjusts them. The corpus grows along the reader's frontier rather than trying to swallow the whole genre.

10.3 The multi-user seam

Everything user-specific ‚ÄĒ ledger, ratings, profile, range position, standing rules ‚ÄĒ lives behind a single userId key from day one, even while that key is constant. The future edition swaps Dexie for hosted Postgres + auth (and gains cross-device sync as the same feature), while the recommender, feature space and seed corpus are shared infrastructure. Because every account already starts from zero and the first run (¬ß7.9) assumes nothing about its reader, going multi-user requires no new onboarding design ‚ÄĒ new users simply get the same front door. Each reader gets their own librarian, as chartered in Chapter I.

10.4 Upgrade path: embeddings

When richer representation is wanted, a synopsis-embedding component (precomputed offline for the seed corpus, stored as static vectors) concatenates onto the feature vector of ¬ß5.1. The scoring pipeline is representation-agnostic, so this is an additive experiment ‚ÄĒ measured against the report card's MAE, adopted only if it demonstrably predicts the reader's verdicts better. The system's own honesty chart decides.

Chapter XIII

Appendix: calibration & glossary

A. The calibration deck

The deck exists so that any reader, starting from nothing, can give the model a defensible shape in a few minutes. Titles are chosen against three criteria: together they must span the twelve axes (so any taste polygon can be sketched), each must be widely read (so recognition ‚ÄĒ and therefore rating rather than skipping ‚ÄĒ is likely), and they must vary in length and register (so runtime and accessibility preferences surface too). An illustrative twenty: Project Hail Mary, The Martian, We Are Legion (We Are Bob), Children of Time, The Player of Games, A Fire Upon the Deep, Blindsight, The Long Way to a Small, Angry Planet, Recursion, Dark Matter, Old Man's War, The Left Hand of Darkness, Seveneves, To Sleep in a Sea of Stars, The First Fifteen Lives of Harry August, All Systems Red, Hyperion, The Three-Body Problem, Annihilation, Dune. Rating even half of them, in any combination, gives the twelve-dimensional polygon enough structure for the model to make its first honest predictions ‚ÄĒ and skipped cards cost nothing.
